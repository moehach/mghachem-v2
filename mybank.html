myBank

Preface

MyBank is a fully digital bank that lives in your wallet. It is a truly borderless bank that combines the benefits of owning a bank account, and offers helpful insights to build financial awareness.

Due Diligence

Brief Requirements

We're creating a fully digital bank. All operations of the bank should be entirely mobile for the customer.

As this app does not have an existing context, the following are the assumptions I made:

  1. The bank is fully digital and will have no physical presence.
  2. Anyone can open a digital account with MyBank, as long as they provide the required documentation.
  3. MyBank is legally allowed to operate and accept clients around the world.
  4. MyBank has found a method to conform to KYC and FATF requirements.

The Problem

Sanctions are in place to limit the possibility/liquidity of money laundering, and criminal activities.

As a result, banks are required to do their own due diligence to make sure their customer are not suspect. This causes banking experiences to be tedious.

More often than not, when you visit a bank, what you, as a customer, might think should only take 5 minutes, ends up taking a whole hour.

What do digital customers look for?

Effortless account authorization

Utilize biometrics to allow quick account access.

Due to privacy and security concern, we will use a multi-factor security solutions for large transactions and specific actions.

All financial insights at a glance

We should deliver spending insights that provide real value to empower users. After opening the application, the user should be able to confidently answer the question:

How am I spending compared to a month ago?

Financial Overview

Studies indicate users only really care to check their balance when they initially open their applications.

The data presented should be simple, easy to understand, and must importantly: useful.

Make transaction history more valuable.

Allows users to see their spending habits, search through their transactions, categorize, and filter them.

Monitor wealth

Users should have instant visibility. If you have multiple accounts, securities, or financial assets, you should be able to gather insight on your overall wealth without bring out a calculator.

Guiding principles

Clear

We're going to get rid of any unncessary data for the average person, and only deliver what matters.

Simple

The interface cannot be the steal the show - The user's financial situation is the star.

Functional

Easy to use without creating overarching or complex flows. Functional also means if an element is on the screen, then it must have a purpose.

Minimal Viable Product

We have a clear picture of who our primary users are, and it's now time to understand what features we will need to create a minimum viable product. For this specific project, I used the "MoSCoW" method to create a clearer image of the MVP.

Must-Have

The features listed under "Must Haves" are required or else the project is going to fail. The presence of these features is non-negotiable.

  • Account Overview
  • Transaction History
  • Card Management
  • User Account settings
  • Money Transfer
  • Seamless account opening
  • Clear and transparent KYC procedures

Should-Have

The features listed under "Must Haves" are required or else the project is going to fail. The presence of these features is non-negotiable.

  • Currency exchange
  • Credit cards
  • Analytics
  • Budgeting
  • Physical cards
  • Multi-lingual support

Could-Have

"Could Haves" are desirable features that aren't crucial for the product launch. These can be removed from the project scope if the time frame is limited for the current build.

  • Cyrptocurrency support
  • Add/send money through NBFIs
  • Access cross-border money transfer services
  • Top up account via ApplePay, Bitcoin
  • Closely manage accounts for children
  • Single use cards (disposable)
  • Stocks and securities (e.g; ETFs, indices)
  • Non-security financial instruments (e.g; Insurance)
  • Personal Loans
  • Referral Points

Won't-Have

"Won't Haves" are the least critical features at the current time but may be considered for future releases.

  • Account ownership transfer
  • Anonymous bank transfers
  • Any feature that could potentially conflict with FATF AML/CFT requirements.

The Design Process

Sign up flow

Perhaps the most crucial part of a banking digital app isn't the app itself, but the sign up flow. Traditional banks don't necessarily have to go through painstaking efforts to verify user identity since they would have already done that when the user first opened an account.

We need to make sure our sign up flow is easy, light-hearted, but most importantly - Efficient for the bank to use.

I recommend we use Machine Learning algorithms here to take care of the KYC part.

Meeting KYC Requirements

To make KYC compliance smoother I'd suggest using a machine learning algorithm to teach the computer how to verify and check data rather than depend on the human eye. This also allows the bank to truly be available 24/7 for new account creation.

If the algorithm flags an image as false, faulty, or otherwise unacceptable, users will be prompted to re-submit their documents or to submit additional information. If the user fails to pass the check a second time, a human is alerted for manual verification to make sure everything is in order.

My understanding is we can give customers temporary approval pending the verification of their documents. We can allow users to use the app but have limited access to it, and restrictive deposit limits for their funds (in other words, users will not be able to deposit a value greater than $1000 (or any value deemed acceptable by the bank specialists).

Wireframes

Now that we know how our pieces will fit together for the first iteration, it's time to begin to create wireframes to see how they translate into a mobile application.

Prototype

On-boarding

User on-boarding screen will be limited to first app installation, or if a user has been logged out for inactivity.

The on-boarding should contain well written copywriting that explains the benefits of the app.

We'll avoid using actual usage instructions since, according to many UX research centres (NNGroup primarily), on-boarding makes interfaces appear harder than they actually are.

Reason?
On a psychological level: If something needs instructions, then it must be hard.

Users might end up second-guessing themselves even when the interface is fairly clear.

Sign up flow

Users a prompted to enter their phone numbers initially. Accounts are directly linked to the numbers. If a user enters a previously entered phone number, then s/he is directed to enter their pass code to access their account.

Otherwise, if a user is registering a new account, the user is asked to assign a pass code that will be used to authenticate the user.

Whenever a screen appears with a field, the first field will instantly be set to active, and the on-screen keyboard will be triggered to get things going.

The KYC requirements are dealt with during the sign up flow.

Users will be prompted to take a photo of a government ID and take a selfie.

This will allow us to satisfy ID, biometric, and face verification as per KYC requirements.

Re-assuring users at this stage that their documents and photos are safe and secure.

Initial access

When initially approved, users will be given restricted access to explore the account.

They will be notified that they need to provide proof of address to fulfill the last bit of KYC compliance. Notification will be sent via push notification, and in-app alerts.

Analytics and Budgeting

Tracking

Users can access their account analytics from their account overview. Users can still switch between different account within this screen simply by tapping on the balance. Users can set a budget to help them take control of their spending habits. Possible feature: Hard limits. This option allows users to disable transactions temporarily if they go over budget.

Budgeting

When a user sets a budget, they can track their progress and see how close they are to reaching their limit.

Push notifications are key to making sure Budget mode is successful. Copywriting is key here.

Transactions

Users can change the time scale of their analytics report and choose between a weekly, monthly, or a yearly report. The transactions section allows users to sort their transactions by categories, merchants/vendors, and countries. Each transaction record has a small bar that visually represents the weight of each transaction record on a user's expenses.

Final Thoughts

Good experience design is not the same as designing a beautiful interface. We forget all too often that the experience is happening not on the phone but rather in our minds. Our mind is on a constant lookout for our well-being, even when all that we are doing is seemingly risk-free.

Making an interface easy to use, easy to understand, and easy to navigate will help set the user at ease. It tells them that you aren't trying to hide something from them.

Experience design is about setting the mind at ease and answering the questions a customer might have. You might answer these questions textually, visually, or psychologically, but what's most important is that you answer these questions honestly because a user will ultimately always know the truth sooner or later. Treating your users with respect and honesty will allow you to create a long-lasting relationship with them. Trying to cheat your users through dark UX patterns might work the first time, but they will quickly learn from that experience to never trust your platform again.

Good experience design, just like good design, is innovative, functional, clean, and most importantly: honest.
